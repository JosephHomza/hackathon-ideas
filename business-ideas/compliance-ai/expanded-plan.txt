Compliance AI Expanded Plan

Goal
Build a compliance product that reduces repetitive work, shortens sales and audit cycles, and gives customers defensible evidence trails they can trust. The business should start with one painful workflow, prove measurable ROI fast, then expand into adjacent compliance tasks.

Decision rule
If we want the best chance of success, we should start with a wedge that:
- has a clear buyer
- happens repeatedly
- is expensive when done slowly
- produces documents, evidence, or approvals that can be tracked
- can be human-reviewed before full automation

Plan A: Vendor security review and audit evidence automation

Target customer
Mid-market B2B SaaS companies, fintech startups, and security-conscious vendors that regularly answer security questionnaires, collect audit evidence, and respond to customer due diligence requests.

Core problem
Small teams lose huge amounts of time gathering screenshots, policy documents, access logs, and control evidence for audits, enterprise procurement, and vendor reviews. The work is repetitive, deadline-driven, and easy to mess up.

Product wedge
A workflow tool that ingests policies, screenshots, CSV exports, cloud settings, SOC 2 artifacts, and questionnaires, then maps them to controls and produces evidence packets, status dashboards, and draft responses.

Pricing idea
- Starter: $500 to $1,500 per month for one team and limited workflows
- Growth: $2,000 to $5,000 per month for stronger integrations and audit trails
- Enterprise: annual contract with custom pricing, onboarding, and dedicated support

Competitive edge
The edge is not generic AI. It is traceability, proof, and speed. Every answer should link back to source evidence. The product should make it easy to see what is missing, who approved it, and when it was last updated. If we can connect directly to systems like Google Workspace, AWS, Jira, Slack, and document stores, we reduce manual chase work and become part of the operating system for compliance.

MVP scope
- Upload policies, screenshots, spreadsheets, and questionnaires
- Extract key fields and map them to a control framework
- Flag missing or stale evidence
- Draft questionnaire responses with source citations
- Track tasks, reviewers, and approval history
- Export a clean audit packet or customer response packet

Go-to-market
Sell to heads of security, compliance, and operations at companies that are too small to build a compliance team but big enough to lose deals if they fail security reviews. Use the pain of customer questionnaires and annual audits as the entry point, then expand to broader compliance workflows.

Launch steps
1. Interview 20 to 30 target buyers and collect real questionnaires, audit evidence lists, and failure stories.
2. Pick one narrow workflow, ideally vendor security reviews or audit evidence collection.
3. Build a concierge pilot where the product helps with one live customer process end to end.
4. Measure hours saved, turnaround time, and number of missing items caught before submission.
5. Turn the best pilot into a repeatable onboarding and export flow.
6. Use outcomes from the first customers to sell the next five.

Risks
- The market can feel crowded if we stay too generic.
- Customers may demand integrations and trust signals early.
- AI mistakes can damage confidence if outputs are not reviewed.
- Sales can slow down if the product tries to cover too many frameworks too soon.

Why it might win
This is one of the strongest compliance wedges because it is concrete, recurring, and tied to revenue. It helps customers close deals and pass audits faster, which is easy to justify. If we become the system that assembles evidence and responses, expansion into policy tracking and control monitoring becomes natural.

Plan B: Continuous policy, control, and governance management

Target customer
Compliance teams, legal ops teams, internal audit groups, and security governance teams at regulated companies and enterprise SaaS vendors.

Core problem
Policies, controls, and approvals are often scattered across docs, emails, spreadsheets, and ticketing tools. Teams struggle to keep them current, align them with regulations, and show auditors a clean story.

Product wedge
A policy and control workspace that tracks documents, ownership, review dates, regulatory mappings, and approval history. The system should summarize changes, detect stale items, and assign follow-up tasks automatically.

Pricing idea
- Team tier: $1,000 to $3,000 per month
- Department tier: $5,000 to $10,000 per month
- Enterprise tier: annual contract with integrations, SSO, and custom reporting

Competitive edge
The product wins by being the best source of truth for policies and controls. Instead of just storing documents, it should enforce ownership, review cadence, and evidence linkage. If the tool can tell a team which policies are outdated, which controls lack evidence, and which approvals are missing, it becomes operational rather than archival.

MVP scope
- Policy repository with versioning
- Control catalog with ownership and review dates
- AI summaries of policy changes and missing sections
- Task assignment and approval workflow
- Regulatory mapping tags
- Audit-ready export with history and citations

Go-to-market
Start with teams already spending time on audits, ISO 27001, SOC 2, vendor reviews, or internal governance. Position the product as a compliance operating system for organizations that need structure more than content generation.

Launch steps
1. Map how compliance documents are currently stored and reviewed.
2. Build a single source of truth for policies and controls.
3. Add change detection and review reminders.
4. Pilot with one team that has recurring review cycles.
5. Prove that the system reduces stale policies and missed approvals.
6. Expand to adjacent compliance and governance tasks.

Risks
- This can become a slow sales motion if buyers think of it as a document repository.
- The product may feel abstract unless tied to clear deadlines and obligations.
- Competing point solutions may already cover parts of the workflow.

Why it might win
This has strong retention if it becomes the canonical place where policies live, get reviewed, and get audited. It is less flashy than AI automation, but it can become sticky and valuable once embedded in recurring governance processes.

Plan C: AI governance and model risk management for businesses using AI

Target customer
Companies deploying internal AI tools, customer-facing AI features, or regulated AI decision support systems. Best first buyers are SaaS companies, fintechs, health tech firms, and larger enterprises rolling out AI governance policies.

Core problem
Companies are adopting AI quickly but lack a simple system for approvals, model inventory, policy compliance, risk reviews, and documentation. Leaders need a way to prove what AI systems exist, how they are used, and what controls are in place.

Product wedge
A lightweight AI governance registry that tracks AI use cases, owners, model sources, risk scores, approval status, testing evidence, and policy exceptions. The product should help teams document AI deployments before a regulator, auditor, or enterprise customer asks for proof.

Pricing idea
- Small team tier: $750 to $2,000 per month
- Governance tier: $3,000 to $7,500 per month
- Enterprise tier: annual contract with policy templates, workflows, and audit exports

Competitive edge
The edge is timing. Most companies know they need AI governance, but few have a simple system in place. If the product is easier to adopt than heavyweight enterprise risk software, and more concrete than a policy PDF, it can win the early market. A focused registry plus workflow can become the practical layer between legal, security, and product teams.

MVP scope
- AI use-case registry
- Owner, purpose, vendor, and model tracking
- Risk classification and approval workflow
- Test and evaluation evidence upload
- Policy exception tracking
- Simple dashboard for leadership and auditors

Go-to-market
Sell to companies with active AI rollouts and visible governance pressure. Target legal, compliance, security, and product operations. The best initial pitch is not "manage AI risk forever" but "document and approve your AI use cases before you scale them."

Launch steps
1. Interview companies already using AI in customer workflows or internal operations.
2. Identify what they must prove to customers, auditors, or internal risk committees.
3. Ship a registry with approvals and evidence tracking first.
4. Add policy templates and reporting only after the registry is useful.
5. Use one or two real AI deployments as showcase case studies.
6. Expand into monitoring, renewals, and exception management.

Risks
- The category is early, so buyers may be curious but not urgent.
- The market may move as regulations and standards evolve.
- If the product is too generic, it can be dismissed as a spreadsheet replacement.

Why it might win
This is a strong bet if AI governance becomes mandatory inside companies. It can ride the growth of internal AI adoption and become a standard control layer. The upside is high because the market is still forming, but the risk is that urgency may be weaker than in audit or vendor review use cases.

Recommendation
If the goal is maximum chance of success, start with Plan A. It has the clearest pain, the most immediate buyer urgency, and the easiest path to measurable ROI. Plan B is a good expansion path once we own evidence and compliance workflows. Plan C is attractive for long-term positioning, but it is riskier as a first product because the buying urgency is less consistent.

Suggested founder strategy
- Build Plan A first
- Use the same evidence and workflow engine to power Plan B later
- Add Plan C only after the product already has trust, workflow, and compliance credibility

Success criteria for the first 90 days
- 10 to 20 serious buyer interviews completed
- 3 to 5 live design partners
- 1 narrow workflow shipped end to end
- 1 measurable outcome that saves time or reduces failure risk
- 1 repeatable onboarding path
- 1 clear reason to expand to the next compliance use case
